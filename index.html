PPhase II: Methods (1920-1947)


5. June 1920 - Non-Cooperation Movement. summary: The campaign rallied against three main issues: the Jallianwala Bagh massacre, the harsh peace treaty Britain and its allies had imposed on the Ottoman Empire, and the injustices of colonial rule. Gandhi identified these three “wrongs” to unite diverse groups under one movement.

6. February 1922 - Chauri Chaura to protest Summary: In February 1922, Indian peasants gathered in the town of Chauri Chaura to protest Britain’s unfair economic policies. Police officers fired upon the crowd and arrested some of the protesters. A crowd of peasants then lit the local police station on fire, killing the twenty-two police officers inside.

7. March 12, 1930, - Salt March Summary: On March 12, 1930, Gandhi began the Salt March to the coast of Dandi. Gandhi began the march with a few dozen people, but by the time he reached the shore, tens of thousands of Indians had joined in the 240-mile trek.

8. March, 1931, - bri-ish agreement with ghandi Summary: In March 1931, Gandhi reached an agreement with the British. Gandhi would call off Civil Disobedience and the British would release the majority of political prisoners and remove the salt tax.

9. 1933, - The Pakistan Movement Summary: Pakistan was first introduced in 1933 as a title for the Muslim community in the northwest provinces of Punjab, North-West Frontier Province (the Afghan border), Kashmir, Sind, and Baluchistan. The Muslim League later promoted Pakistan as a political identity that connected Muslims across all provinces.

10. 1935 - Government of India Act. Summary: The law was intended to appease some groups, while maintaining tight control over the colony.

11. 1940 - Lahore Resolution. Summary: At the Muslim League’s 1940 annualconvention in Lahore, a city in Punjab, Jinnah demanded that Muslims be recognized as a nation deserving of its own “homelands.” Jinnah’s declaration became known as the Lahore Resolution.

12. 1947 - Freedom and Transfer of power Summary: On June 3, 1947, Mountbatten planned to announce the terms of British withdrawal, including the fate of Bengal, on the All-India Radio to the Indian public.
